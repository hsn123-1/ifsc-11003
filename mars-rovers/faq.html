Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions about the rovers themselves, the planet they explore, and the people who keep them running. Expand any question to read the answer.

About the Rovers

What is a Mars rover?

A Mars rover is a robotic, wheeled vehicle designed to travel across the Martian surface. Unlike stationary landers, rovers can drive to new sites, examine rocks and soil with onboard instruments, and return images and data over months or years of operation.

Why does NASA send rovers instead of astronauts?

Sending robots is significantly cheaper, safer, and faster than sending people. A rover doesn't need food, air, or return transportation, and it can be designed to survive radiation and extreme temperatures that would be hazardous to humans. Robotic missions also help scout and prepare for future human exploration.

What powers a Mars rover?

It depends on the rover. Early rovers like Sojourner used solar panels, which work well but are affected by dust and winter sunlight. Larger rovers like Curiosity and Perseverance use a radioisotope thermoelectric generator (RTG), which produces electricity from the natural decay of plutonium-238 and operates reliably day and night, in any season.

Why can't rovers drive faster?

Rovers drive slowly, typically well under 0.2 km per hour, to protect their expensive instruments from rocks, slopes, and soft sand. Drivers on Earth also need time to plan each route from images, and communication delays of 5 to 20 minutes each way mean rovers can't be steered in real time.

About Mars

How long does it take to travel from Earth to Mars?

Typical transfer orbits take about six to nine months, depending on the relative positions of Earth and Mars at launch. Launches are usually timed to windows that occur roughly every 26 months, when the two planets are best aligned for an efficient trajectory.

Has a rover ever found life on Mars?

No. However, rovers have found strong evidence that ancient Mars had the conditions needed to support microbial life, including liquid water, a stable climate over long periods, and the chemical building blocks of life. Perseverance is collecting samples that could one day be returned to Earth for more detailed analysis.

What is a "sol"?

A sol is a Martian day, measured from one solar noon to the next. A sol lasts about 24 hours, 39 minutes, and 35 seconds, just slightly longer than an Earth day. Mission teams often work on "Mars time" during the early phase of a mission, shifting their schedules to match.

Mission Operations

How do rovers communicate with Earth?

Rovers send most of their data through orbiters like the Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter, which relay signals to NASA's Deep Space Network of radio antennas on Earth. They can also communicate directly, though at much slower data rates.

What happens to a rover when its mission ends?

Rovers typically stop operating when power is lost, communications fail, or a critical system wears out. Once silent, they remain where they are on the surface. Sites like Pathfinder's landing area have been officially renamed, for example, to "Carl Sagan Memorial Station."

Are any rovers still active on Mars?

Yes. As of today, both Curiosity and Perseverance continue to operate on the Martian surface, returning images and scientific data. Their missions have each been extended well beyond their original planned lifetimes.
