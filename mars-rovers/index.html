Exploring the Red Planet

Four decades of discovery, one wheeled explorer at a time.

Since 1997, NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory has landed a growing family of robotic rovers on the surface of Mars. Each one has extended our understanding of the planet's geology, climate, and potential to have once supported life. This site introduces three of those missions and the people, engineering, and science behind them.

The Mars Rover Program

The Mars Rover Program is a long-running effort by NASA to explore the surface of Mars using mobile, instrumented robots. Each mission builds on the last: Sojourner proved that wheeled exploration on another planet was possible, the Mars Exploration Rovers dramatically extended surface operations, Curiosity brought a full geochemistry laboratory, and Perseverance is now caching rock samples for eventual return to Earth.

Missions are designed, built, and operated by NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory in partnership with universities and industry, and the data they return is made publicly available to researchers and citizens worldwide.

The Rovers

Mars Pathfinder · 1996–1997

Sojourner

Mission Goals

As NASA's first successful Mars rover, Sojourner's primary goal was to demonstrate that mobile exploration on Mars was technically feasible. Secondary goals included analyzing rocks and soil near the landing site and returning weather data.

Launch & Landing

Launched aboard a Delta II rocket from Cape Canaveral on December 4, 1996. The Pathfinder spacecraft carrying Sojourner landed at Ares Vallis on July 4, 1997, using an airbag-cushioned bouncing landing.

Discoveries

Sojourner analyzed more than fifteen rocks and returned evidence that Mars was once warmer and wetter than it is today. It also collected atmospheric and weather data, returning over 550 images during its mission.

Technical Features

At roughly 10.6 kg and about 65 cm long, Sojourner was solar-powered and carried an Alpha Proton X-ray Spectrometer to measure the composition of rocks. Designed for a seven-sol mission, it operated for 83 sols before contact was lost.

Mars Science Laboratory · 2011–present

Curiosity

Mission Goals

Curiosity was designed to assess whether Mars ever had the environmental conditions needed to support microbial life. It studies the climate and geology of Gale Crater and characterizes the radiation environment in preparation for future human missions.

Launch & Landing

Launched aboard an Atlas V rocket on November 26, 2011. Curiosity landed inside Gale Crater on August 6, 2012, using a rocket-powered "sky crane" descent — a maneuver nicknamed the "Seven Minutes of Terror."

Discoveries

Curiosity confirmed that ancient Mars had persistent liquid water and the chemical building blocks needed for life. It has detected complex organic molecules preserved in rock and measured seasonal variations in methane in the atmosphere.

Technical Features

About the size of a small car (899 kg, roughly 3 m long), Curiosity is powered by a radioisotope thermoelectric generator. It carries 17 cameras, a rock-vaporizing laser instrument (ChemCam), and the SAM chemistry laboratory, and remains operational today.

Mars 2020 · 2020–present

Perseverance

Mission Goals

Perseverance is searching for signs of ancient microbial life in Jezero Crater, collecting and caching rock and soil samples for eventual return to Earth, and testing technologies that will support future human exploration of Mars.

Launch & Landing

Launched aboard an Atlas V rocket on July 30, 2020. Perseverance landed in Jezero Crater on February 18, 2021, again using the sky crane descent system pioneered by Curiosity.

Discoveries

Perseverance has identified organic molecules in Jezero's ancient lake-bed sediments and sealed multiple rock-core samples for a future Mars Sample Return mission. Its MOXIE instrument also produced breathable oxygen from the Martian atmosphere, and its Ingenuity helicopter companion completed the first powered flight on another planet.

Technical Features

At about 1,025 kg, Perseverance is the largest rover ever sent to Mars. It carries 23 cameras and two microphones — the first instruments to record audio on another planet — along with a suite of science instruments and the SHERLOC and PIXL spectrometers on its robotic arm.
