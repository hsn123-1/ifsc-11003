What's Calculus All About?

Calculus is the branch of mathematics dedicated to understanding how things change. At its core, it gives us the tools to analyze motion, growth, and transformation. This can be used to gather information on the speed of a falling object, the spread of a population, or the curve of a rollercoaster. Calculus is built on two big ideas: derivatives, which measure how quickly something is changing at any given moment , and integrals, which measure the total accumulation of change over time. Together, these concepts allow us to describe and predict the behavior of almost anything that moves or evolves, making calculus one of the most powerful and widely used tools in science, engineering, economics, and beyond.

A Brief History of Calculus

Origins and Development

Calculus was independently developed in the 17th century by two of history's greatest minds: Isaac Newton and Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz. Newton developed his version, which he called "the method of fluxions," primarily to solve problems in physics and motion, while Leibniz developed his own notation, the one we still use today. Newton himself captured the spirit of scientific discovery when he remarked, If I have seen further, it is by standing on the shoulders of giants. The impact of their work cannot be overstated. Calculus became the foundation of modern science and engineering, prompting mathematician Richard Courant to reflect on its significance:

Calculus is the most powerful weapon of thought yet devised by the wit of man.

The Newton-Leibniz Controversy

Despite both men arriving at calculus independently, the question of who deserved credit sparked one of history's most bitter disputes in mathematics. Newton had developed his ideas as early as the 1660s but delayed publishing them, while Leibniz published his own work in 1684. Supporters of each mathematician accused the other of plagiarism, and the debate divided the English and Continental European mathematical communities for decades. Today, historians widely accept that both men developed calculus independently. Ironically, it is Leibniz's notation, not Newton's, that mathematicians still use worldwide.

Timeline

  1. Ancient Greece (c. 400 BC) — Early ideas of infinity and limits emerge through Zeno's paradoxes and Archimedes' method of exhaustion, laying the philosophical groundwork for calculus.
  2. Medieval Islamic Mathematics (c. 1000 AD) — Scholars like Ibn al-Haytham develop early methods of integration to calculate volumes and areas.
  3. Early 17th Century — Pierre de Fermat and Rene Descartes develop analytic geometry and methods for finding tangents to curves, directly preceding calculus.
  4. 1665-1666 — Isaac Newton develops his "method of fluxions" during a period of isolation caused by the Great Plague, though he does not publish his work.
  5. 1684-1686 — Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz publishes his version of calculus, introducing the notation that mathematicians still use today.
  6. 18th Century — Leonhard Euler and others expand calculus significantly, applying it to physics, astronomy, and engineering.
  7. 19th Century — Augustin-Louis Cauchy and Karl Weierstrass formalize calculus through rigorous definitions of limits, putting the subject on solid mathematical foundations.

Topics Glossary

  • Differential Calculus
    Limits
    The value a function approaches as its input gets closer to a given point.
    Derivatives
    A measure of how a function changes with respect to a change in its input.
    Slopes of Curves
    The steepness of a curve at any given point, found using the derivative.
    Chain Rule
    A rule for differentiating composite functions by multiplying their derivatives.
  • Integral Calculus
    Riemann Sums
    An approximation of the area under a curve using a series of rectangles.
    Definite Integrals
    The exact area under a curve between two specified points.
    Area Under a Curve
    The total accumulated value of a function over a given interval.
    Integration By Parts
    A technique for integrating the product of two functions.
  • Multivariable Calculus
    Partial Derivatives
    The derivative of a function with respect to one variable while holding others constant.
    Multiple Integrals
    Integrals extended over two or more dimensions to find volumes and areas.
    Optimization in 3D
    Finding maximum or minimum values of functions with multiple variables.
    Lagrange Multipliers
    A method for finding extrema of a function subject to constraints.
  • Vector Calculus
    Gradient
    A vector that points in the direction of the steepest increase of a function.
    Divergence
    A measure of how much a vector field spreads out from a given point.
    Curl
    A measure of the rotation or circulation of a vector field at a given point.
    Line Integrals
    An integral computed along a curve, used in physics to calculate work done by a force.
  • Differential Equations
    ODEs
    Ordinary differential equations involve functions of a single variable and their derivatives.
    PDEs
    Partial differential equations involve functions of multiple variables and their partial derivatives.
    Initial Value Problems
    Differential equations solved with a specified value of the function at a starting point.
    Laplace Transform
    A technique that converts differential equations into simpler algebraic equations.
